Section III

Program structure

Over the next five weeks you will research, design, and present an activity for the Harland training app. Ms. Ruby's project is different. She will design a complete onboarding experience for new families. An activity is a specific thing a student does inside the app to build a skill outside of their lessons. You are not writing code. You are defining what your activity should do, how it should work, and what it should look like, clearly enough that a developer can build it from your brief.

You will try things that do not work. That is how this kind of work goes. Often noticing what does not work is more valuable than finding what does.

What an activity brief is

A short document that tells a developer exactly what to build. It covers purpose, how the activity works step by step, what content goes inside, and any open questions. Specific enough that a developer can build from it without asking follow-up questions. If they could read it and start coding, it is done.

The standard template with examples is available as a reference page. Open it before you begin your brief in week 4. Link will be shared in the Claude Project.

What a draft looks like

A strong draft shows three things. First, the core experience. What a student actually does when they use your activity. Second, a sample of real content, enough for someone to try it. Third, your thinking about why it works.

Form is up to you. Google Docs, slides, a written document, sketches, whatever best communicates your activity.

Aim for 2 to 3 pages or equivalent. Hard ceiling at 4. If you cannot fit it in that space, you are not yet clear enough on what your activity is.

Section VI

How critique works

In week 4 each of you will review a colleague's draft and give them written feedback. This is where the real pedagogical thinking gets sharpened. Done well it makes the work better, builds trust, and teaches you to think critically about your own project by examining someone else's.

You are not there to approve. You are there to improve.
Critique pairings
Ms. MreviewsMr. Glen's Vocabulary Journal
Mr. GlenreviewsMs. M's K–3 Reading Room
Mr. JudkinsreviewsMs. Ruby's New Family Experience
Ms. RubyreviewsMr. Judkins' SAT Practice Room
Format

Written. Shared with your colleague before the Tuesday standup of week 4. No more than one page. Be direct. Vague praise is not useful to anyone.

What to look for
  • Clarity. Can you explain what the activity is in one sentence after reading the draft? If not, that is the first thing to flag.
  • Assumptions. What is being taken for granted? What would a student, parent, or teacher challenge?
  • Strengths. What is the single best idea in the draft? Name it specifically.
  • One question. The single most important question the draft has not yet answered.

The assignment

You know better than anyone where new parents and students get confused, what questions they ask, and where the current experience falls short. Your five areas of work
  • 1. Journey mapDocument every single thing that happens to a new family between signing up and completing their first lesson. Every message, every document, every question. Do not design yet. Just map what currently happens and where the gaps are.
