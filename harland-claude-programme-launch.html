From the Principal
To the Harland Team,

We are at an inflection point — not just as a company, but as educators. The tools available to us today would have been unimaginable five years ago, and the teams that learn to use them well will define what premium education looks like in the decade ahead.

This programme is my invitation to each of you to become part of that. Not as passive observers of what AI can do, but as active designers of how Harland will use it.

Over the next five weeks, each of you will take ownership of a real project — one that will ultimately shape the learning experience we deliver to our students. You will work with Claude as a thinking partner, a research assistant, a design collaborator, and a critic. You will present your work to the team. And you will leave this programme with a capability that will change how you work, permanently.

I have chosen each of your projects deliberately. They reflect what you know, what you are best positioned to build, and where I believe Harland needs your voice most. I am not asking you to become technologists. I am asking you to bring your expertise to a new tool — and to trust that the combination is more powerful than either alone.

I am genuinely excited to see what this team produces.

Mr. Harris
Principal · Harland Education

Standup log

How critique works
You are not there to approve. You are there to improve.
In week 2 each of you will review a colleague's research and give them written feedback. This is one of the most important parts of the programme. Done well it makes the work better, builds trust, and teaches you to think critically about your own project by examining someone else's.
What to look for
Gaps. What is missing from the research? What platform or approach should they have looked at?
Assumptions. What are they taking for granted? What would a student or parent challenge?
Strengths. What is the strongest insight in their research? Name it specifically.
One question. The single most important question they have not yet answered.
Format
Written. Shared with your colleague before the Tuesday standup. No more than one page. Be direct — vague praise is not useful to anyone.

Your project
The New Family Experience
You know better than anyone where new parents and students get confused, what questions they ask, and where the current experience falls short. This project takes that knowledge and turns it into something every new Harland family will experience — a complete, designed journey from enrolment confirmation through to the end of their first lesson.
Your project is intentionally broader than the others. It draws on operational knowledge rather than pedagogical research — which means you are the expert in the room and no one else can do what you are doing.
New families
Journey design
Communications
Your knowledge
Your five areas of work
1. Journey map. Document every single thing that happens to a new family between signing up and completing their first lesson. Every message, every document, every question. Do not design yet — just map what currently happens and where the gaps are.
2. Communication sequence. Design the ideal sequence of messages a new family should receive. What arrives the day they enrol? The day before their first lesson? After their first lesson? What channel — LINE, email, or app notification? Write the actual copy for each touchpoint.
3. Training app introduction. Write a clear, friendly introduction to the Harland training app — what it is, what they will find inside, how to get started. Then design how this would be delivered inside the app itself, screen by screen.
4. FAQ. The top ten questions parents ask and the top ten questions students ask. Clear, warm answers to each. These will live in the app and be used by Student Coordinators.
5. Coordinator handoff brief. When a new family is assigned to a Student Coordinator, what does that coordinator need to know immediately? Design the one-page summary — what it contains, how it is formatted, when it is delivered.
Week 1 focus
Begin with what you already know. Map the current new parent and student journey from first contact through first lesson. Where do questions come up? Where does confusion happen? What do people ask that they should not need to ask? Document this before researching what others do. Your operational experience is the most valuable input here.
Your week 1 deliverable
A written journey map of the current new family experience — every touchpoint, every common question, every gap you have observed. Bring this to Tuesday's standup.
Week 3 shared task
Contribute two principles to the team's shared Harland Training Activity standards document. Your principles should draw from what you know about how new students and parents experience the app — what every good Harland activity should feel like to a family encountering it for the first time.
